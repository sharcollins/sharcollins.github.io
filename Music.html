How Effective is Music Therapy?

Techniques Used In Music Therapy

The definition of music is vocal or instrumental sounds (or both) combined in such a way as to produce beauty of form, harmony, and expression of emotion. The definition of therapy is the treatment of mental conditions through verbal communication and interaction.

Music therapy is a form of treatment that uses music to address the physical, emotional, cognitive, and social needs of individuals. It has been shown to be effective in a variety of settings, including hospitals, schools, and community centers, and with individuals of all ages. Music therapy is the clinical, evidence-based use of musical treatments to meet certain goals in a therapeutic setting by a qualified individual who has successfully completed a certified music therapy program.

There have been numerous research studies conducted on the effectiveness of music therapy. One such study, published in the book Music Therapy: An Art Beyond Words states that the pleasure gained from listening; the warmth and friendship from being part of a group making music; the stimulus and satisfaction from regular practice and rehearsal; the intellectual delight from exploring the intricacies of musical forms and structures; the physical energy released within us by both playing and listening to music, inspiring us often to move and dance.{1} This source gives a background look at how music helps many different people and the things they are battling. For example, Mental health. As stated in this source “Music helps us to feel more human. It brings us into very close and immediate contact with the people around us and at the same time connects us both with images from the past and predictions of the immediate future.” {1}

Other studies have also found that music therapy can be effective in improving communication skills, reducing pain, and improving quality of life in individuals with various medical conditions, such as cancer, stroke, and Parkinson's disease.

Some common techniques used in music therapy include:

  • Improvisation: This involves creating music at the moment, without any prior planning or preparation. It allows individuals to express themselves and explore their emotions in a safe and non-judgmental environment.
  • Songwriting: This involves writing and creating original songs, which can be used to express feelings, share experiences, and communicate thoughts and ideas.
  • Receptive music therapy: This involves listening to music in a structured way, and using it as a tool for relaxation, stress reduction, and emotional expression.
  • Instrumental music therapy: This involves playing musical instruments, either individually or in a group, to achieve therapeutic goals.
